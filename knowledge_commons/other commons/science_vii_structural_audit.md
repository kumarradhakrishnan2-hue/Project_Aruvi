### Chapter 1 — The Ever-Evolving World of Science

**Chapter identification:** Chapter 1, 6 pages. Orientation chapter — not a content chapter in the standard sense. No "Let Us Enhance Our Learning" exercise section.

**Task architecture inventory:**
- **Activity 1.1 "Question the Answer"** | Embedded in body text | Operation: student generates a science question for each of five given answers (e.g., answer: "It is the same on all sides" → student must write a question) | Cognitive operation: divergent question-generation from a given constraint | Requires: application to unfamiliar context (most answers are abstract, not about memorised content) | Stand-alone.

No end-of-chapter exercise. No Exploratory Projects section.

**Conceptual task profile:** No exercise section; not applicable.

**Procedural task profile:** No physical procedures. Activity 1.1 is entirely cognitive.

**Investigative task profile:** Activity 1.1 is the closest to an investigative orientation in the book — it requires the student to reason about what makes a valid scientific question — but it does not involve data generation or experimental design.

**Dissolution test signal:** If the question-generation activity is removed, the chapter has no developmental layer at all — it becomes a purely descriptive orientation text. The single cognitive operation the chapter asks of the student is: generating a scientific question from an observed or described phenomenon. No second operation is present.

**Constitutional note:** Chapter 1 is not mappable through the standard competency architecture. It has no exercise, no lab activity, and no factual content tested. Its sole developmental task is metacognitive. This chapter should be flagged as architecturally distinct and handled separately in the constitution.

### Chapter 2 — Exploring Substances: Acidic, Basic, and Neutral

**Chapter identification:** Chapter 2, 16 pages.

**Task architecture inventory:**

*Embedded activities:*
- **Activity 2.1 "Let us explore"** | Embedded, body text | Collect household samples; test each with red and blue litmus paper; record colour change and classification in provided table | Operation: procedural execution of a testing protocol; guided observation with table-based recording | Requires: procedural execution + guided observation | The procedure is fully described; the student follows it. Part of a progression — establishes the classification framework used throughout the chapter.
- **Activity 2.2 "Let us relate and explore"** | Embedded, body text | Given a list of foods, identify the chemical name of the acid each contains | Operation: application of classification to a familiar context using provided reference information | Requires: recall + application to familiar context | Stand-alone.
- **Activity 2.3 "Let us prepare"** | Embedded, body text | Prepare a red rose petal extract indicator following described steps | Operation: procedural execution — physical preparation of a chemical solution | Requires: procedural execution | Part of a progression (indicator prepared here is used in 2.4).
- **Activity 2.4 "Let us find out"** | Embedded, body text | Test household samples with rose extract indicator; record colour changes in table; classify each sample | Operation: procedural execution of testing protocol using student-prepared indicator | Requires: procedural execution + guided observation | Extends 2.1 — same classification task, different indicator.
- **Activity 2.5 "Let us prepare"** | Embedded, body text | Prepare turmeric indicator paper following described steps | Operation: procedural execution | Part of progression — another indicator preparation task.
- **Activity 2.6 "Let us investigate"** | Embedded, body text | Test olfactory indicators (onion, vanilla) with acid and base samples; record observations | Operation: procedural execution + guided observation | Requires: procedural execution + guided observation | Stand-alone variant of indicator testing.

*End-of-chapter exercise ("Let Us Enhance Our Learning"), ~10 questions:*
- Identify the incorrect statement about neutralisation reactions from a set of four — requires evaluation of conceptual claims: **conceptual evaluation (application)**
- Match acid + corresponding base → neutralisation product — **recall**
- Fill-in-the-blank (indicator colours) — **recall**
- Practical reasoning: safe/unsafe to store bleaching powder near citric acid — requires causal chain reasoning about neutralisation: **application + causal explanation**
- Scenario: baking soda used for indigestion — explain why — **causal explanation (familiar context)**
- Scenario: red cabbage juice (new, unfamiliar indicator) changes colour in a solution — interpret the observation — **application to unfamiliar context (transfer)**
- Real-world problem: bee sting (formic acid) vs wasp sting (alkali) — what household remedy to apply, and why — **application to unfamiliar context + justify**
- Design activity: given a mystery substance, describe how to test whether it is acidic, basic, or neutral — **procedural design (semi-investigative)**
- Research and collect local plant-based indicators; test them; document colour changes — **investigative design (Exploratory Project category)**

**Conceptual task profile:** End-of-chapter exercise is mostly application-demanding with causal reasoning. Only 2 of ~10 questions are pure recall. One question (red cabbage) requires transfer to an unfamiliar indicator context — the highest-demand conceptual task in the set. No question requires causal explanation of the mechanism of indicator action (why colour changes) — the mechanistic level is not tested.

**Procedural task profile:** 6 embedded activities requiring physical execution of described procedures. All procedures are provided to the student step-by-step; the student executes them but does not design them. One exercise question requires the student to design (not just execute) a testing procedure — this is the only point where procedural design is demanded. The chapter teaches the procedures explicitly as content (how to prepare and use indicators).

**Investigative task profile:** No embedded activity requires the student to independently formulate a hypothesis or design a method. Activity 2.1 and 2.4 involve guided observation with a prediction element (before-and-after colour), but the prediction is implicit (the table prompts it). The Exploratory Project (collect and test local plant indicators) has genuine investigative openness — the student chooses materials and must document and interpret results — but it is outside the body chapter structure.

**Dissolution test signal:** Remove the ability to test substances with indicators and classify them as acidic, basic, or neutral — the chapter loses its reason for existing as a teaching unit. The central cognitive operation is: **classifying substances using chemical indicators through systematic procedural testing**. A secondary conceptual thread (neutralisation as a reaction between acids and bases) is present but is not structurally dominant in the task architecture — most tasks support classification, not reaction mechanism reasoning. No genuine co-central.

### Chapter 3 — Electricity: Circuits and their Components

**Chapter identification:** Chapter 3, 18 pages.

**Task architecture inventory:**

*Embedded activities:*
- **Activity 3.1 "Let us explore"** | Embedded | Examine a torch: operate the switch; open it to observe interior components | Operation: guided observation of component layout | Requires: guided observation | Initiates component-identification progression.
- **Activity 3.2 "Let us observe"** | Embedded | Examine an electric cell; identify positive and negative terminals | Operation: guided observation + label identification | Requires: guided observation | Part of component-identification progression.
- **Activity 3.3 "Let us experiment"** | Embedded | Insert cells in torch in different orientations; observe which makes it work | Operation: systematic trial-and-error to discover polarity rule | Requires: guided observation + procedural variation | Generates a rule from student-executed trials.
- **Activity 3.4 "Let us observe"** | Embedded | Examine incandescent lamp; identify filament and its electrical connections | Operation: guided observation + component identification | Requires: guided observation.
- **Activity 3.5 "Let us observe"** | Embedded | Examine LED; compare structure to incandescent lamp | Operation: comparative guided observation | Requires: guided observation + application of classification.
- **Activity 3.6 "Let us construct"** | Embedded | Build a complete working electrical circuit: connect electric cell, lamp, wires, holders | Operation: physical construction of a functional device following described layout | Requires: procedural execution (construction) | This is the core procedural task of the chapter — the student produces a working circuit for the first time.
- **Activity 3.7 "Let us experiment"** | Embedded | Make an LED glow using a battery; discover by trial that LED has polarity requirement | Operation: procedural trial-and-error + guided observation → rule derivation | Requires: procedural execution + guided observation.
- **Activity 3.8 "Let us construct"** | Embedded | Construct a simple switch using described materials | Operation: physical construction of a functional component | Requires: procedural execution (construction).
- **Activity 3.9 "Let us test"** | Embedded | Insert constructed switch into circuit; verify it opens and closes the circuit | Operation: testing a constructed component in a real system | Requires: procedural execution + verification | Part of construction progression.
- **Activity 3.10 "Let us explore"** | Embedded | Test various objects as the conductor segment in a circuit; record result (lamp glows / does not glow) in table; classify as conductor or insulator | Operation: systematic procedural testing → classification | Requires: procedural execution + guided observation + classification | Stand-alone classification task.
- **Activity 3.11 "Let us design and create"** | Embedded | Design and build a circuit-based quiz board or similar interactive application | Operation: open design + construction | Requires: independent procedural design | Highest-order procedural task in the chapter — student must translate a functional goal into a circuit layout.

*End-of-chapter exercise ("Let Us Enhance Our Learning"), 11 questions:*
- Identify the incorrect statement (four statements about circuits/components) — **recall + conceptual evaluation**
- Diagram: which material in the gap will break the circuit? — **application (familiar context)**
- Diagram: if one bulb in a series circuit has a broken filament, which other bulbs glow? Give reason — **causal reasoning (application)**
- Why are wires covered with insulating material? — **causal explanation (familiar context)**
- Draw a circuit diagram for a described set-up — **procedural (symbolic representation)**
- Multi-part diagram: given four positions of switch S, state whether lamp L glows in each — **systematic application**
- Lamp in a circuit does not glow: list all possible reasons — **divergent causal reasoning (application to familiar context)**
- Given four circuit diagrams, identify in which case the lamp will not glow — **application (conceptual analysis)**
- Design a method to identify which terminal of an unmarked battery is positive — **semi-investigative design**
- Design an activity: a box contains 12 cells, some working and some not — how would you identify which are working? — **genuine investigative design (student must design method, not follow one)**
- Identify the error in a drawn circuit diagram and redraw it correctly — **application (conceptual correction)**

**Conceptual task profile:** Exercise is application-dominant. Only 1 of 11 questions is pure recall. Five questions require causal reasoning (series circuit logic, insulation purpose, fault diagnosis). Two questions require procedural output (drawing a circuit diagram, designing an activity). One question (Q10) is the most cognitively demanding: student must independently design a multi-step method with no scaffolding.

**Procedural task profile:** 7 of 11 embedded activities require physical construction or systematic execution. Two activities involve genuine construction tasks producing functional devices (circuit, switch). Activity 3.11 requires the student to design a circuit application — this is the only body-text activity where the student determines the procedure independently. The chapter explicitly teaches construction procedures (it builds circuit competency incrementally across activities 3.6–3.9).

**Investigative task profile:** No body activity is genuinely investigative. Activity 3.3 and 3.7 involve trial variation, but the expected outcome is stated in the text. Activity 3.11 has design freedom but remains within defined circuit construction. Q9 and Q10 in the exercise are the genuine investigative design tasks — they require the student to propose a method for an untested problem. Q10 is the strongest investigative signal in the chapter (design a method to solve an identification problem using only available materials).

**Dissolution test signal:** Remove the ability to construct, analyse, and troubleshoot a working electrical circuit — the chapter dissolves. The central cognitive operation is: **constructing and reasoning about the behaviour of complete electrical circuits by applying knowledge of components, polarity, conductor/insulator classification, and open/closed circuit states**. No second operation is genuinely co-central.

### Chapter 4 — The World of Metals and Non-metals

**Chapter identification:** Chapter 4, 16 pages.

**Task architecture inventory:**

*Embedded activities:*
- **Activity 4.1 "Let us explore"** | Embedded | Test malleability: hammer a piece of metal (aluminium foil or copper wire) and a non-metal (carbon rod, sulphur) | Operation: procedural execution + guided observation; compare results to classify | Requires: procedural execution + guided observation | Initiates the property-testing progression.
- **Activity 4.2 "Let us investigate"** | Embedded | Drop metal vs non-metal objects onto a hard surface; compare sound produced | Operation: guided observation of sonority as a distinguishing property | Requires: guided observation | Part of property-testing progression.
- **Activity 4.3 "Let us investigate"** | Embedded | Compare heat conduction: hold metal and non-metal rods, heat one end, observe temperature change at other end | Operation: guided observation of conductivity as a distinguishing property | Requires: guided observation + guided comparison.
- **Activity 4.4 "Let us design and create"** | Embedded | Design and construct a circuit-based conductivity tester; use it to test a set of given materials; record results; classify as conductors or non-conductors; then connect to the metals/non-metals distinction | Operation: construction of a functional device + systematic testing protocol + classification | Requires: procedural execution (construction) + systematic testing + guided inference | This is the most complex activity in the chapter — it requires prior knowledge from Chapter 3 and applies it as a tool for a new classification task.
- **Activity 4.5 "Let us experiment"** | Embedded | Set up four test tubes: iron nail in (a) open air, (b) boiled water + oil, (c) dry air with silica gel, (d) salt water; observe over several days | Operation: multi-condition procedural set-up with longitudinal observation; student records change (rusting) across conditions; infers which condition(s) are necessary | Requires: procedural execution + longitudinal guided observation + inference from multi-condition data | This is the most investigative activity in the chapter — the student must set up four conditions and reason about which variables cause rusting.

*End-of-chapter exercise ("Let Us Enhance Our Learning"), ~11 questions:*
- Identify the odd one out from each group (classify by property category) — **recall + classification**
- Match property to example substance — **recall**
- True/false statements about metal and non-metal properties — **recall + conceptual evaluation**
- Practical scenario: choose material for cooking utensil; justify using property knowledge — **application to familiar context + causal reasoning**
- Explain why gold is used for jewellery and copper for electrical wiring — **causal explanation (familiar context)**
- Real-world problem: preventing iron articles from rusting — list methods and explain why each works — **causal reasoning + application**
- Given a new material with a described set of properties: classify as metal or non-metal and justify — **application to unfamiliar context (transfer)**
- Design a test using available materials to determine if an unknown substance is a metal or non-metal — **semi-investigative design**
- Environmental context: why is recycling metals important? — **application in cross-curricular context**

**Conceptual task profile:** Exercise is split between recall (3 questions) and application with causal reasoning (5 questions). One transfer question (unfamiliar material classification) is the highest-demand conceptual task. No question requires explanation of the mechanism behind properties (e.g., why metals conduct electricity at the atomic level) — the chapter stays at the observational/functional level.

**Procedural task profile:** 4 of 5 activities require physical execution. Activity 4.4 requires the student to construct a device AND design a systematic testing protocol using it — this is the highest-order procedural task in the chapter. Activity 4.5 requires multi-day longitudinal observation with a controlled set-up. The chapter teaches the testing procedures explicitly as part of "how to identify a metal."

**Investigative task profile:** Activity 4.5 has multi-condition experimental design (4 conditions for rusting), but the conditions are described — the student sets them up but does not choose them. The inference required (which condition is necessary for rusting) is genuine but guided by the table structure. Exercise Q8 (design a test for unknown material) is a genuine semi-investigative design task. No activity requires the student to independently formulate a hypothesis.

**Dissolution test signal:** Remove the ability to systematically test material properties and classify substances as metals or non-metals — the chapter dissolves. Central cognitive operation: **identifying and applying the physical and chemical properties of metals and non-metals to classify an unknown material**. The rusting sub-topic is a special case within the metals property framework (chemical property: reactivity with oxygen and water), not a co-central thread. Clean single.

### Chapter 5 — Changes Around Us: Physical and Chemical

**Chapter identification:** Chapter 5, 16 pages.

**Task architecture inventory:**

*Embedded activities:*
- **Activity 5.1 "Let us think and reflect"** | Embedded | Given a list of 10 everyday changes (candle melting, paper burning, milk souring, etc.); student observes or recalls and records in a table: what changed, is it reversible, and type | Operation: classification of changes using guided observation and recall | Requires: recall + guided classification | Initiates the physical/chemical distinction.
- **Activity 5.2 "Let us create and discuss"** | Embedded | Perform simple reversible changes (fold paper, inflate/deflate balloon, crush chalk); then perform an irreversible change (burn paper); observe and classify reversibility | Operation: procedural execution + guided observation | Requires: procedural execution + guided observation | Extends 5.1 with physical execution.
- **Activity 5.3 "Let us explore"** | Embedded | Blow air through lime water; observe colour change; compare to blowing through tap water | Operation: procedural execution + guided observation | Requires: procedural execution + guided observation | Introduces CO₂ detection; sets up chemical change context.
- **Activity 5.4 "Let us experiment"** | Embedded | Mix vinegar and baking soda in a bottle; attach a balloon over the mouth; observe inflation | Operation: procedural execution + guided observation; inference question: "Is this change reversible?" | Requires: procedural execution + guided observation + causal inference | The inference question (reversibility of gas production) is the only element requiring reasoning beyond observation.
- **Activity 5.5 "Let us investigate"** | Embedded | Set two identical candles burning; cover one with a glass jar; observe which extinguishes first | Operation: procedural execution + guided observation; conclusion: oxygen required for burning | Requires: procedural execution + guided observation | Procedure and expected outcome are both described.
- **Activity 5.6 "Let us investigate"** | Embedded | Attempt to light paper with matchstick vs with focused sunlight (magnifying glass); observe the difference | Operation: procedural execution + guided observation; introduces ignition temperature concept | Requires: procedural execution + guided observation.
- **Activity 5.7 "Think, pair, and share"** | Embedded | Students read a dialogue between two fictional students debating whether burning a candle involves physical or chemical change; analyse both positions | Operation: interpretation of a multi-perspective text argument; student must evaluate competing claims | Requires: application to familiar context + causal evaluation | This is the only activity in the chapter requiring reasoning about competing explanations rather than observation.

*End-of-chapter exercise ("Let Us Enhance Our Learning"), ~11 questions:*
- Complete a classification table (changes → physical or chemical, reversible or irreversible) — **recall + application**
- Match example → type of change — **recall**
- Causal explanation: why cannot the burning of paper be reversed? — **causal reasoning**
- Describe what happens when wood burns (list all observations) — **application to familiar context with multi-aspect description**
- True/false with justification: "all chemical changes are irreversible" — **application + conceptual correction**
- Candle burning: argue whether this is physical, chemical, or both; justify each aspect — **multi-step causal reasoning (application to familiar context)**
- Identify the odd one out from a list of changes — **recall + application**
- Scenario: a student mixes two household liquids and observes bubbles — what type of change? How to confirm? — **application + semi-investigative design**
- Application to unfamiliar context: a new material dissolves in water and the temperature drops — physical or chemical? Justify — **transfer to unfamiliar context**

**Conceptual task profile:** Exercise is application-dominant with strong causal reasoning demand. Only 2 of ~11 questions are pure recall. Three questions require multi-step causal explanation. One transfer question (unfamiliar material with temperature change) demands independent application of the classification criteria to a new context. The candle-burning question (physical + chemical simultaneously) is the highest-demand conceptual task — it requires holding two simultaneous explanatory frames.

**Procedural task profile:** 5 of 7 activities require physical execution of described procedures. None require the student to design a procedure. The procedures are vehicles for the conceptual goal — no activity teaches technique as content (unlike Chapters 2, 3, 4 where indicator preparation and circuit construction are content goals in themselves).

**Investigative task profile:** No activity is genuinely investigative. All procedures are described and expected outcomes are stated. One exercise question (bubbles observation) requires a semi-investigative design response (how to confirm the change type), but the procedure itself is not designed by the student in the activity.

**Dissolution test signal:** Remove the ability to distinguish physical from chemical changes based on evidence of new substance formation — the chapter dissolves. Central cognitive operation: **classifying observed changes as physical or chemical by identifying evidence for or against new substance formation, and reasoning about the reversibility criterion**. No second operation is co-central.

Stand-alone.

- **Activity 6.4 "Let us spread awareness"** | Embedded | Group task: produce a poster on responsible social media use during adolescence; complete a table of dos and don'ts | Operation: synthesis + communication design | Requires: application to social context + creative production | Stand-alone.

*End-of-chapter exercise ("Let Us Enhance Our Learning"), 10 questions:*
- Scenario: a friend develops pimples — list probable reasons and suggest remedies — **causal reasoning + application to familiar context**
- Given two food choices (pictorial): which is healthier during adolescence? Justify — **application to personal health context with causal reasoning**
- Unscramble terms related to adolescence — **recall (word puzzle)**
- Evaluate a claim: "adolescence involves only physical changes" — correct it with examples — **conceptual correction + application**

**Procedural task profile:** No physical procedures in any embedded activity. This is the only chapter in NCERT VII Science with zero laboratory or physical execution tasks. Activities are discussion, listing, and poster design. The procedural competency type is structurally absent from this chapter's body activities.

**Investigative task profile:** No investigative tasks. The metacognitive Q5 (generate questions to test claims) has an inquiry orientation but does not require experimental design or evidence interpretation.

**Dissolution test signal:** Remove the ability to explain the biological, emotional, and social changes that occur during adolescence and trace their causes — the chapter dissolves. Central cognitive operation: **explaining the causes and significance of physical, hormonal, emotional, and social changes during puberty, and reasoning about their implications for health and behaviour**. This is a single explanatory framework despite covering multiple domains (biological, psychological, social). No genuine co-central.

**Constitutional note for Q2:** Chapter 6 demonstrates that procedural competency is structurally impossible to assign for some chapters. Any constitution that treats procedural competency weight as universally applicable will produce mapping failures on this chapter.

### Chapter 7 — Heat Transfer in Nature

**Chapter identification:** Chapter 7, 16 pages.

**Task architecture inventory:**

*Embedded activities:*
- **Activity 7.1 "Let us experiment"** | Embedded | Metal strip with wax pins at marked positions; heat one end over candle; predict which pin falls first; observe | Operation: prediction + procedural execution + guided observation; prediction precedes outcome — student commits before observing | Requires: application to familiar context (prediction) + procedural execution + guided observation | Part of the conduction progression.

- **Activity 7.4 "Let us investigate"** | Embedded | Fill two identical containers with soil and water; place thermometers in each; record temperatures at regular intervals as both are heated; plot data in Table 7.4 | Operation: quantitative data collection over time + data recording; student must then compare rates | Requires: procedural execution + quantitative data collection + comparison | This is the only activity in the chapter requiring the student to collect and record numerical time-series data. The comparison of heating rates is a genuine inference task.
- **Activity 7.5 "Let us investigate"** | Embedded | Place two identical strips (one black, one white) in sunlight; place thermometers on each; compare temperatures after a set time | Operation: controlled comparison — procedural execution + guided observation of differential heating | Requires: procedural execution + guided observation + comparison.

*End-of-chapter exercise ("Let Us Enhance Our Learning"), ~11 questions:*
- Identify mode of heat transfer for each described scenario (hand over fire, boiling water, sunlight warming a room) — **recall + application to familiar context**
- Causal explanation: why does a metal spoon in hot soup heat up faster than a wooden spoon? — **causal reasoning (familiar context)**
- Application: which material should be used for a cooking pot handle, and why? — **application to familiar context + causal justification**
- Predict: what happens when very cold water is added to the bottom of a container of warm water? — **application of convection concept to a novel arrangement**
- Explain: why do dark-coloured objects warm up faster in sunlight? — **causal reasoning (familiar context)**
- Real-world application: explain why a sea breeze blows from sea to land during the day — **causal chain reasoning with unfamiliar multi-step application**

**Procedural task profile:** All 5 activities require physical execution of described procedures. None require the student to design a procedure in the body text. Activity 7.4 is the most sophisticated procedural task — quantitative time-series data collection with a comparison inference. The chapter teaches procedures as vehicles for concept development; the techniques themselves are not the content.

**Investigative task profile:** No activity is independently investigative. Activity 7.4 involves data collection the student generates themselves and must compare, which has an investigative quality, but the conditions, variables, and expected conclusion are all specified. The exercise design question (Q9) is the closest to genuine investigative design in the chapter.

**Dissolution test signal:** Remove the ability to identify the correct mechanism of heat transfer (conduction, convection, radiation) for a given scenario and use that mechanism to explain observed phenomena — the chapter dissolves. Central cognitive operation: **applying the correct heat transfer mechanism to explain the direction, rate, and outcome of heat movement in natural and everyday contexts**. No genuine co-central.

### S1 — Task Architecture Patterns

**The dominant architecture** across NCERT VII Science (excluding Chapter 1) is a three-layer structure:
1. **Embedded activity layer:** 4–10 activities within the body text, each with a boxed title (e.g., "Let us explore," "Let us investigate," "Let us construct"). Activities are procedural, constructive, or observational. The textbook provides the procedure; the student executes or observes.
2. **End-of-chapter exercise layer ("Let Us Enhance Our Learning"):** 10–12 questions spanning a cognitive range from recall to unfamiliar-context transfer. The exercise is the primary site of application-demanding, causal-reasoning, and investigative-design tasks.
3. **Exploratory Projects layer:** 2–4 open-ended tasks following the exercise; the most investigatively open tasks in the textbook. These are frequently semi-investigative (student has design choices) but are not assessed in the standard sense.

**10 of 11 content chapters** follow this three-layer architecture. The exception is Chapter 1 (orientation), which has only one embedded activity and no exercise.

**Outlier chapters that break the dominant pattern:**

- **Chapter 1** (The Ever-Evolving World of Science): No exercise, no lab activities, single metacognitive task. Cannot be mapped using standard competency architecture. Architecturally distinct.

This is the only chapter where the procedural competency type is structurally absent from the activity layer. The exercise is the sole site of causal and application reasoning.
- **Chapter 8** (Measurement of Time and Motion): Architecture present, but exercise is dominated by formula-application calculation (7 of 11 questions) rather than causal-conceptual reasoning. This is the only chapter where the primary exercise operation is numerical-procedural rather than explanatory.
- **Chapter 10** (Life Processes in Plants): Architecture present, but the activity layer is split: 4 of 8 activities are teacher demonstrations (not student-executed). The student-executed procedural proportion is significantly lower than all other laboratory-style chapters.
- **Chapter 12** (Earth, Moon, and the Sun): Longest chapter (22 pages). Activities include 2 longitudinal real-world data collection tasks (newspaper + sky observation over days/weeks) that are unlike any other activity in the book. The Exploratory Projects in this chapter have the strongest investigative structure of any chapter's projects.

- **Constitutional implication for Q2:** Investigative competency as a body-activity type is structurally very limited in NCERT VII Science. If a constitution assigns investigative weight based on body activities, it will severely over-estimate investigative presence. The realistic locus of investigative competency in this textbook is: (a) 1 exercise question per chapter in approximately 7 of 11 chapters, and (b) Exploratory Projects. ### S4 — The Descriptive Layer Boundary

**In most chapters**, the descriptive layer (explanatory body text) and the developmental layer (student-facing tasks) are structurally distinguishable through typographic markers: activities are boxed with headers; body text between them is expository. This separation holds cleanly in Chapters 2, 3, 4, 5, 7, and 11.

**Five boundary-blurring patterns** emerge across the textbook:

**Pattern 1 — Diagram-as-embedded-task (Chapters 11, 12):** Several body-text figures have questions embedded beneath them (e.g., "In Fig. 12.17, how many hours of sunlight does the North Pole receive on 21st June?"). The diagram is formally in the descriptive layer but contains a cognitive task — the student must read and extract data from it. This is a developmental task embedded in the descriptive layer. In Chapter 11, body-text figures showing ray diagrams invite the student to trace paths, which is the chapter's central cognitive operation. These tasks are not separately boxed and would not be captured by an activity inventory alone.

**Pattern 2 — Body-text data tables used as exercise material (Chapters 9, 10):** In Chapter 10, Bhaskar's experiment result table (Table 10.2) is presented in the body text as descriptive content, but it becomes the object of exercise Q6 and Q8. The descriptive layer functions as a pre-loaded data set for the developmental layer. The boundary here is temporal, not structural.

**Pattern 3 — Embedded thought prompts in body text (Chapters 6, 9, 12):** Several chapters include italicised questions or thought-bubble prompts in the body text (e.g., "Can you think of other examples?", "What do you think would happen if…?"). These are not in the activity layer but are developmental in function. They are invisible to an activity inventory. They are most frequent in Chapters 6 and 9.

**Pattern 4 — "Think Like a Scientist" extension boxes (Chapter 8):** Appearing after Activity 8.2, this box extends a guided procedure into a variable-investigation task. It is typographically part of the activity layer but operationally it asks the student to do something structurally different from the parent activity (systematic variable investigation vs. single measurement). It sits at the boundary between guided procedural and semi-investigative.

**Pattern 5 — "Science and Society," "Holistic Lens," "Fascinating Facts" boxes:** These are explicitly informational boxes in the descriptive layer, but some (particularly Holistic Lens boxes in Chapters 2, 7, and 8) contain an implicit application question or cross-disciplinary reasoning prompt that the exercise then formalises. They prime the developmental layer task without being part of it.

**Constitutional implication for S4:** A constitution that defines the developmental layer as "only formally marked activities and end-of-chapter exercises" will miss Pattern 1 (diagram tasks), Pattern 3 (body-text thought prompts), and Pattern 5 (informational boxes with embedded task orientation). The most constitutionally significant of these is Pattern 1: in Chapters 11 and 12, the primary cognitive operation of the chapter (ray tracing; spatial model reasoning) is practised through body-text figures as much as through the formal exercise. A constitution that weights competency types purely from the exercise and formal activities will undercount the developmental load carried by diagrams in these chapters.
