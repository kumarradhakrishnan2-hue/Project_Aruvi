Chapter 7 — Heat Transfer in Nature

Chapter 7 introduces students to the concept of heat transfer and explores how heat moves through
different media, driving natural phenomena such as wind, sea breezes, and the water cycle. The
chapter examines three distinct modes of heat transfer — conduction, convection, and radiation —
and shows how each operates in the natural environment.

The chapter begins by distinguishing heat from temperature. Temperature is a measure of the
degree of hotness or coldness of a substance and is measured in degrees Celsius (°C) or degrees
Fahrenheit (°F). Heat is a form of energy that flows from a region of higher temperature to a
region of lower temperature. Students are introduced to thermometers as the standard instruments
for measuring temperature, including laboratory thermometers and clinical thermometers. The
chapter notes the relationship between the two temperature scales and introduces the Celsius scale
as the standard for scientific work.

Section 7.2 introduces conduction as the mode of heat transfer through solids. When one part of
a solid is heated, its particles gain energy and vibrate faster; they transfer this energy to
neighbouring particles, and heat spreads progressively through the material. An embedded activity
attaches pins to a metal strip using wax at regular intervals; when one end is heated by a candle
flame, the pins drop sequentially from the heated end outward, vividly demonstrating the
progressive conduction of heat along the metal. Students discover that different materials conduct
heat at different rates: metals are good thermal conductors, while wood, rubber, glass, and
plastic are poor conductors (thermal insulators). This explains the design of everyday objects
such as cooking vessels (metal base, insulating handle) and thermos flasks (vacuum between glass
walls to prevent conduction).

Section 7.3 introduces convection as the primary mode of heat transfer in fluids — liquids and
gases. When a fluid is heated, the heated portion becomes less dense and rises while cooler,
denser fluid descends to take its place, establishing a circulation pattern called a convection
current. An embedded activity uses potassium permanganate crystals placed at the bottom of a
beaker of water: when heated from below, purple plumes rise from the crystals and spread
outward, making the convection current visible. The chapter connects convection to important
meteorological phenomena. During the day, land heats up faster than the adjacent sea; warm air
over the land rises, and cooler air from the sea flows in to replace it, producing a sea breeze.
At night, land cools faster than the sea; the now relatively warmer air over the sea rises, and
cooler air from the land flows seaward, producing a land breeze. On a larger scale, global
convection currents in the atmosphere driven by differential solar heating of the equatorial and
polar regions produce the trade winds and other global wind patterns.

Section 7.4 introduces radiation as heat transfer through electromagnetic waves, which can travel
through a vacuum without requiring any medium. All objects emit thermal radiation; the amount
emitted increases with temperature. Dark, dull surfaces are more efficient absorbers and emitters
of radiation than light, shiny surfaces. This explains why solar cookers and passive solar
heating systems use dark-coloured surfaces to maximise heat absorption, and why light-coloured
clothing is preferred in hot sunny conditions to reflect rather than absorb radiation from the Sun.

Section 7.5 connects the three modes of heat transfer to the water cycle. Solar radiation heats
the surface of oceans, lakes, and rivers, causing evaporation. The resulting water vapour rises
by convection into the cooler upper atmosphere, where it condenses around dust particles to form
clouds. When droplets in clouds grow large enough, they fall as precipitation — rain, snow, or
hail. This cycle continuously redistributes both water and thermal energy around the planet,
sustaining the fresh water supply and influencing local and global climates.

An extended investigation spanning multiple days asks students to compare the rates at which
different natural surfaces — soil, sand, and gravel — absorb and release water, connecting the
physical properties of surface materials to the rates of evaporation and the behaviour of water
in the environment.

By the end of the chapter, students can explain each of the three modes of heat transfer, give
examples of each from natural and technological contexts, and connect the mechanisms of heat
transfer to large-scale natural phenomena including wind, land and sea breezes, and the water
cycle.
