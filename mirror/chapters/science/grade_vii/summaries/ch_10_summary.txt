Chapter 10 — Life Processes in Plants

Chapter 10 examines the major life processes that sustain plant life: photosynthesis, the
transport of water and dissolved substances, respiration, and a brief treatment of excretion. The
chapter establishes plants as autotrophs that produce their own food from inorganic raw materials
using light energy, and explores how the products of photosynthesis sustain not only the plant
itself but the broader ecosystem.

Section 10.1 introduces photosynthesis as the defining life process of green plants. Using
sunlight, carbon dioxide absorbed through stomata in the leaves, and water absorbed through the
roots, green plants synthesise glucose and release oxygen as a by-product. The green pigment
chlorophyll, located in chloroplasts in plant cells, captures light energy and drives this
synthesis. The word equation is clearly stated: Carbon dioxide + Water → (light energy,
chlorophyll) → Glucose + Oxygen.

A series of investigations examines the conditions necessary for photosynthesis. A long-term
activity grows three potted plants under different conditions — one in sunlight with water, one in
sunlight without water, and one in darkness with water — and after two weeks tests leaves from
each plant for starch using iodine solution (blue-black colour indicates starch is present). A
teacher demonstration shows the correct procedure for preparing a leaf for starch testing: boiling
the leaf in water to disrupt cells, decolourising it in hot alcohol to remove chlorophyll, and
rinsing and applying iodine. Further teacher-demonstrated experiments investigate the role of
carbon dioxide (using a plant sealed in a bottle with carbon dioxide-absorbing caustic soda) and
the role of light (using a leaf partially covered with black paper to create a variegated pattern
of light and dark zones). The results of these investigations establish that light, water, and
carbon dioxide are all necessary inputs for photosynthesis to occur.

Section 10.2 addresses the transportation of materials within the plant. Water and dissolved
minerals are absorbed from the soil by root hair cells and transported upward through the xylem
— a network of elongated, hollow, dead cells that form continuous tubes from roots to leaves. An
embedded activity demonstrates water transport by placing a plant stem in coloured water and
observing, the next day, that the colour has spread through the xylem into the leaves. The
upward movement of water is driven primarily by the transpiration stream: the continuous
evaporation of water vapour through stomata creates a negative pressure that pulls water upward
through the xylem. Dissolved food (sucrose produced by photosynthesis) is transported in both
directions through the phloem — a second set of vascular tubes — to supply all growing and
storage regions of the plant.

A teacher-demonstrated activity prepares a thin cross-section of a leaf and examines it under
magnification to show the stomata — the tiny pores through which gas exchange and transpiration
occur — each guarded by two bean-shaped guard cells that control the size of the pore opening.

Section 10.3 introduces respiration in plants. Like all living organisms, plants respire
continuously at the cellular level to extract energy from glucose. During the day, the rate of
photosynthesis far exceeds the rate of respiration, so the plant is a net producer of oxygen.
At night, only respiration occurs, and the plant is a net consumer of oxygen and producer of
carbon dioxide. A teacher-demonstrated experiment places germinating seeds in a sealed flask
connected to a lime water tube; the lime water turns milky as the carbon dioxide produced by
the seeds' respiration reacts with calcium hydroxide, confirming aerobic respiration.

Section 10.4 briefly addresses excretion in plants. Plants do not possess specialised excretory
organs. Metabolic waste products are often stored harmlessly in vacuoles, converted to useful
compounds, or incorporated into structures such as gums, resins, and latex. The oxygen released
during photosynthesis can itself be considered an excretory product, as it is a by-product of the
metabolic process.

The chapter closes by emphasising the ecological significance of plants as the foundation of most
food chains: through photosynthesis, plants capture solar energy and store it in chemical form,
making it available to all the heterotrophic organisms that depend on plants for food.
