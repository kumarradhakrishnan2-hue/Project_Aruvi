Chapter 9 — Life Processes in Animals

Chapter 9 examines the major life processes that sustain animal life — nutrition, respiration,
circulation, and excretion — explaining how each process works at the organ-system level and how
these systems are interconnected to sustain the organism as a whole.

Section 9.1 introduces nutrition in animals. Unlike plants, animals cannot synthesise their own
food and must obtain it by consuming other organisms. The process of animal nutrition comprises
five stages: ingestion (taking food into the body), digestion (breaking food mechanically and
chemically into molecules small enough to be absorbed), absorption (taking digested nutrients
from the alimentary canal into the bloodstream), assimilation (using absorbed nutrients in cells
for growth, repair, and energy production), and egestion (expelling undigested waste).

The alimentary canal is traced from the mouth, where physical chewing and the enzyme amylase in
saliva begin the digestion of starch, through the oesophagus (which conveys food to the stomach
by peristalsis), the stomach (where acid and enzymes break down proteins), the small intestine
(where bile from the liver emulsifies fats and pancreatic enzymes complete the digestion of all
major food groups), and the large intestine (which absorbs water and prepares undigested matter
for expulsion). An embedded activity uses iodine solution to test for the presence of starch in
regular chapati versus chewed or saliva-treated chapati, demonstrating that the enzyme in saliva
chemically transforms starch even before it reaches the stomach.

Section 9.2 introduces respiration, distinguishing it carefully from breathing. Breathing is the
physical mechanism by which air is moved into and out of the lungs; respiration is the cellular
chemical process by which oxygen is used to release energy from glucose, producing carbon dioxide
and water as by-products. The chapter explains the mechanism of breathing: contraction of the
diaphragm and the intercostal muscles increases the volume of the chest cavity, reducing air
pressure inside the lungs so that air flows in; relaxation of these muscles decreases the volume,
expelling air. Gas exchange occurs in the tiny air sacs (alveoli) of the lungs: oxygen diffuses
from inhaled air into the blood, and carbon dioxide diffuses from the blood into the air to be
exhaled. A model of the chest cavity built from a plastic bottle, Y-shaped tube, and two balloons
demonstrates how the movement of the diaphragm produces the pressure changes that drive airflow.

Section 9.3 introduces the circulatory system as the body's internal transport network. The heart
is a four-chambered muscular pump. Oxygen-rich blood is pumped from the left ventricle into the
aorta, distributed through a branching network of arteries to every tissue of the body, and
returned through veins to the right atrium. From the right ventricle, blood is pumped to the
lungs for re-oxygenation, returning to the left atrium. This double circuit — pulmonary
(heart-to-lungs-and-back) and systemic (heart-to-body-and-back) — ensures efficient delivery of
oxygen to tissues and removal of carbon dioxide. Capillaries, the finest blood vessels, form the
dense network in tissues where exchange of oxygen, nutrients, and waste products occurs between
blood and body cells.

Section 9.4 introduces excretion — the removal of the metabolic waste products generated by the
body's chemical processes. The kidneys filter blood, extracting urea, excess salts, and water to
produce urine, which is stored in the bladder and expelled. The lungs excrete carbon dioxide and
water vapour with every exhalation. The skin excretes salts and a small amount of urea in sweat.
The liver plays a central detoxifying role, converting the ammonia produced by protein
catabolism into the less toxic compound urea, which the kidneys then excrete.

By the end of the chapter, students can explain each of the four major life processes in animals
at the organ-system level and describe how these systems are integrated to sustain animal life.
