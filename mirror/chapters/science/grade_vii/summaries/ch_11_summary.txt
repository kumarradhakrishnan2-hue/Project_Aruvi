Chapter 11 — Light: Shadows and Reflections

Chapter 11 investigates the behaviour of light — how it travels, how it interacts with different
materials, how it forms shadows, and how it reflects from surfaces. The chapter develops these
ideas through a large number of hands-on activities and connects the properties of light to the
operation of familiar optical instruments including the plane mirror, the pinhole camera, the
periscope, and the kaleidoscope.

Section 11.1 begins by distinguishing between luminous and non-luminous objects. Luminous
objects — the Sun, electric lamps, candle flames, glowing fireflies — produce and emit their own
light. Non-luminous objects can only be seen because they reflect light from a luminous source:
the Moon, planets, and all ordinary objects around us are non-luminous and visible only in the
presence of a light source. This distinction sets the foundation for all subsequent discussion.

Section 11.2 introduces rectilinear propagation — the observation that light travels in straight
lines. An investigation using three cardboard matchboxes, each with a hole punched through the
centre, demonstrates the principle clearly: light from a candle passes through all three holes
only when they are perfectly aligned. Displacing any box breaks the light path, showing that
light cannot bend around corners. A second activity confirms this with a straight pipe and a bent
pipe: a candle flame is visible through the straight pipe but not through the bent one.

Section 11.3 classifies materials by how light interacts with them. Transparent materials (clear
glass, clean water, air) allow light to pass through freely, enabling clear images to be formed.
Translucent materials (tracing paper, frosted glass, thin cloth) allow light to pass but scatter
it, preventing the formation of clear images. Opaque materials (cardboard, metal, wood) block
light completely. Students systematically test a range of materials using a torch, recording
observations in a table.

Section 11.4 develops the formation of shadows. When an opaque object intercepts a beam of light,
it casts a shadow — a region of darkness beyond the object. With a point source of light, the
shadow has a single dark region (umbra). With an extended source, the shadow has a dark central
region (umbra) surrounded by a partially lit region (penumbra). Students investigate how the size
and sharpness of shadows depend on the size and distance of the source, the size of the object,
and the distance of the object from the surface on which the shadow falls.

Section 11.5 introduces reflection. When light strikes a smooth, shiny surface, it bounces back
in a predictable way governed by the laws of reflection: the angle of incidence equals the angle
of reflection, and the incident ray, the reflected ray, and the normal at the point of incidence
all lie in the same plane. An investigation uses a plane mirror, a torch, and a fine comb to
produce narrow parallel light beams; students observe the reflected beams and measure angles to
verify the first law of reflection.

Section 11.6 explores images formed by a plane mirror. An object placed in front of a plane
mirror forms an image that appears to be located behind the mirror at the same distance as the
object is in front of it. The image is the same size as the object, is upright, and is laterally
inverted — left and right are swapped relative to the object. Students observe this lateral
inversion by writing their names and observing the mirror image, and by examining how letters and
words appear reversed.

Section 11.7 introduces the pinhole camera as a device that exploits rectilinear propagation to
form images. Students construct a pinhole camera from two sliding cardboard boxes with a small
hole on the front face and a tracing-paper screen at the rear. The image formed is real (projected
onto the screen) and inverted: light from the top of the object passes through the pinhole and
reaches the bottom of the screen, and vice versa. The size of the image depends on the distance
of the object from the pinhole and the distance of the screen from the pinhole.

Section 11.8 introduces the periscope — which uses two plane mirrors at 45° angles to redirect
light so that a viewer can see over obstacles — and the kaleidoscope, in which multiple mirrors
arranged in a triangular tube produce beautiful symmetric patterns through repeated reflections.
Students are invited to construct both devices as exploratory projects.

By the end of the chapter, students can explain rectilinear propagation, describe shadow
formation, state and apply the laws of reflection, characterise the image formed by a plane
mirror, and explain the working of a pinhole camera.
