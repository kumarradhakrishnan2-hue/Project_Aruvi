Chapter 6 — Adolescence: A Stage of Growth and Change

Chapter 6 explores adolescence as a distinct stage of human development characterised by
significant physical, hormonal, emotional, and social changes. The chapter describes the
biological mechanisms that drive the transition from childhood to adulthood, and provides guidance
on health and well-being practices that support students through this period.

The chapter opens by defining adolescence as the stage of life bridging childhood and adulthood,
generally spanning from around ten or eleven years of age to eighteen or nineteen. Puberty — the
onset of sexual maturation — marks the beginning of the most intense phase of physical change
within adolescence. The chapter emphasises that the timing of puberty varies considerably between
individuals and begins somewhat earlier in girls than in boys on average; this variation is normal
and expected.

Section 6.1 situates adolescence within the broader arc of human development, distinguishing it
from earlier stages of childhood growth. Students are invited to reflect on the physical and
emotional changes they have themselves observed over the preceding few years and to consider how
these connect to the biological concept of puberty.

Section 6.2 describes the physical changes of adolescence in detail. Growth in height accelerates
during this period, driven by growth hormone secreted by the pituitary gland. Body shape changes
differ between sexes: boys develop broader shoulders and increased muscle mass; girls develop
wider hips as the pelvis widens in preparation for potential childbearing in adulthood. Facial and
body hair appears in both sexes — more prominently in boys. Voice changes are particularly
notable in boys, whose larynx enlarges under the influence of testosterone, causing the voice to
deepen. Skin changes are common in both sexes: the sebaceous glands become more active,
increasing oil production and often leading to acne. Sweat glands also become more active,
producing increased sweating and associated odour.

Section 6.3 introduces the endocrine system and the role of hormones in coordinating the changes
of adolescence. Hormones are chemical messengers produced by endocrine glands and transported
through the bloodstream to act on target organs. The pituitary gland, located at the base of the
brain, is described as the master gland because it secretes hormones that regulate many other
endocrine glands. The thyroid gland produces thyroxine, which regulates the body's overall
metabolic rate and affects growth. The adrenal glands produce adrenaline, which prepares the
body for emergencies by increasing heart rate and releasing stored glucose. The reproductive
glands — testes in males and ovaries in females — produce the sex hormones testosterone and
oestrogen respectively, which drive the development of secondary sexual characteristics and
the maturation of the reproductive system.

Section 6.4 describes the changes in the reproductive system during adolescence. In males, the
testes mature and begin to produce sperm. In females, the ovaries mature and begin to release
eggs; the uterus develops capacity for pregnancy. Menstruation is introduced as a normal
biological process: the uterine lining builds up each month in anticipation of a fertilised egg,
and if fertilisation does not occur, the lining is shed. This cyclic process, regulated by
hormones, constitutes the menstrual cycle and typically spans approximately 28 days. The chapter
treats menstruation matter-of-factly as a healthy biological event and emphasises the importance
of hygiene and comfort during this time.

Section 6.5 addresses the emotional and social dimensions of adolescence. Heightened emotional
sensitivity, mood variability, emerging independence, and shifting relationships with family and
peers are described as characteristic features of this stage, driven partly by hormonal
fluctuations and partly by the expanding social and intellectual world students begin to navigate.

Section 6.6 provides practical guidance on health and well-being. A nutritious diet — rich in
proteins for muscle growth, calcium and phosphorus for bone development, iron to prevent anaemia,
and vitamins for overall health — is essential to support the rapid physical growth of
adolescence. Regular physical exercise promotes fitness, reduces stress, and supports positive
mental health. Personal hygiene, including regular bathing, clean clothing, dental care, and
attention to skin health, is emphasised. The chapter affirms that seeking information from trusted
adults and health professionals, and maintaining a positive outlook on bodily change, are
important foundations for navigating adolescence well.
