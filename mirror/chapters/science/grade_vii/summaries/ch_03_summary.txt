Chapter 3 — Electricity: Circuits and their Components

Chapter 3 explores how electricity flows through circuits and introduces students to the
components that make up electrical circuits and the effects that an electric current can produce.
The familiar electric torch serves as the chapter's central entry point, progressively revealing
the functions of its parts and generalising these to the broader concept of a complete electrical
circuit.

The chapter opens by inviting students to examine a working torch and identify what it consists
of. Through this investigation, three essential components emerge: a source of electrical energy
(the electric cell or battery), a device that uses electricity (the lamp), and conducting
connections (wires). The switch, which controls the flow of current by opening or closing the
circuit, is established as the fourth essential element.

Section 3.1 examines each component in detail. An electric cell converts stored chemical energy
into electrical energy. It has two terminals — a positive terminal marked + and a negative
terminal marked − — and cells can be connected in series to form a battery that provides greater
electrical energy. The torch lamp consists of a thin filament of tungsten wire enclosed in a glass
bulb; the filament glows white-hot when current passes through it. The switch can be in an open
position (circuit incomplete, no current) or closed position (circuit complete, current flows).

Section 3.2 introduces the general concept of an electric circuit — a complete, closed path
through which electric current flows from the positive terminal of the cell, through the external
circuit, and back to the negative terminal. For a lamp to glow, the circuit must be unbroken.
Students build and test simple circuits using a cell, a lamp in a holder, connecting wires, and a
switch, observing how opening or closing the switch controls the lamp.

Section 3.3 introduces standard symbols for circuit components: the cell (two unequal parallel
lines), the battery (multiple cells), the lamp (circle with a cross), the switch (open or closed),
and connecting wires (straight lines). Students practise drawing circuit diagrams using these
symbols and learn to translate between an actual physical circuit and its schematic representation.

Section 3.4 distinguishes electrical conductors from insulators. Students build a circuit with a
gap and insert different materials into the gap to determine whether the lamp glows. Metals —
including copper, iron, and aluminium — are identified as good electrical conductors. Rubber,
plastic, wood, and glass are identified as insulators. Graphite (the material in pencil leads)
is noted as an exception: a non-metal that conducts electricity. The practical significance of
this distinction is clear: copper is used for wiring, while rubber and plastic insulate the
wires to prevent electric shocks.

Section 3.5 introduces two effects of electric current that extend beyond the simple flow of
charge. The heating effect: when current flows through a conductor, electrical energy is
converted to heat energy, causing the conductor to warm up. This principle underlies the
functioning of electric heaters, incandescent bulbs, and fuses. Students observe that the
filament of a lamp reaches high enough temperatures to emit light. The magnetic effect: a
current-carrying wire behaves like a magnet and can attract small iron or steel objects. Students
observe this by passing current through a coil of wire and bringing small iron objects close to
it. The magnetic effect of current is the operating principle of electromagnets, electric motors,
and electric bells.

Throughout the chapter, the sequence of activities leads students from examining familiar objects
(torch, LED, cell, switch) through circuit construction and testing, to abstract representation
(circuit diagrams) and the investigation of electrical properties (conductors, insulators) and
effects (heating, magnetism). By the end, students can describe the components and function of a
simple electrical circuit, draw and interpret circuit diagrams using standard symbols, classify
materials as conductors or insulators, and explain the heating and magnetic effects of electric
current.
