Chapter 5 — Changes Around Us: Physical and Chemical

Chapter 5 examines the changes that matter undergoes, establishing the fundamental distinction
between physical changes — in which the form or appearance of a substance is altered without
producing a new substance — and chemical changes, in which a new substance with different
properties is formed. The chapter develops both categories through everyday examples and embedded
investigations, building towards a coherent conceptual framework for classifying the changes
students observe around them.

The chapter opens by presenting a broad range of familiar changes: folding paper, inflating a
balloon, crushing chalk, dissolving salt in water, burning magnesium, ripening of fruits, and
rusting of iron. Students are invited to consider what these changes have in common or what
distinguishes them. The initial observation is that some changes can be easily reversed while
others cannot: a folded sheet of paper can be unfolded; a burnt piece of paper cannot be restored.
This intuition about reversibility becomes the first diagnostic criterion for the classification
that follows.

Section 5.1 introduces physical changes in detail. In a physical change, the chemical composition
of the substance remains unchanged even though its shape, size, or state may be altered. Tearing
paper changes its shape but not its material identity. Dissolving sugar in water is also a
physical change: the sugar is no longer visible, but it can be recovered by evaporation of the
water, demonstrating that no new substance was formed. Changes of state — ice melting to water,
water evaporating to steam — are also physical changes because the substance involved (water)
remains chemically the same throughout. Physical changes are generally reversible under ordinary
conditions.

Section 5.2 introduces chemical changes. In a chemical change, one or more new substances are
formed with properties that differ from those of the starting materials. The chapter identifies
several observable indicators that a chemical change has occurred: the production of a gas
(evidenced by bubbling or effervescence), a change in temperature (heat released or absorbed),
a change in colour, the formation of a precipitate (an insoluble solid), or the production of
light or sound. An embedded investigation combines vinegar (an acid) and baking soda (a base),
producing vigorous bubbling and the evolution of carbon dioxide gas — clear evidence of a
chemical change. Another investigation involves the burning of a piece of paper: the ash produced
has entirely different properties from the original paper, confirming the formation of a new
substance. Burning candles, the souring of milk, and the rusting of iron are all identified as
chemical changes because they produce new substances that cannot be simply converted back into
the original material.

Section 5.3 extends the analysis to more complex cases. The cooking of food involves chemical
changes: raw egg, bread dough, and raw meat are all chemically transformed during cooking, and
these transformations are irreversible under ordinary conditions. The decay of organic matter and
the digestion of food are also identified as chemical changes with important biological
significance. The chapter also notes that some apparently straightforward cases require careful
thought: dissolving salt in water is physical (salt can be recovered), while dissolving salt with
an acid may involve a chemical reaction depending on the substances involved.

A key conceptual refinement is the treatment of reversibility as a useful but imperfect criterion.
Some chemical changes can be reversed under special conditions (the decomposition of water into
hydrogen and oxygen by electrolysis can be reversed by burning hydrogen in oxygen). Some physical
changes are difficult to reverse in practice (crushing a chalk stick cannot be easily undone).
Students are therefore guided to prioritise the deeper criterion: whether a new substance with
new properties has been formed, rather than relying solely on reversibility.

The chapter closes by connecting the distinction to a particulate model: physical changes
rearrange particles without altering their identity, while chemical changes involve the breaking
and forming of bonds between particles, producing particles that constitute new substances with
different properties. This particle-level explanation provides a coherent theoretical framework
for all the observational evidence developed throughout the chapter.
