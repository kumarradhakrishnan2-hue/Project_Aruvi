Chapter 4 — The World of Metals and Non-metals

Chapter 4 introduces students to the classification of materials as metals and non-metals through
the systematic investigation of their physical and chemical properties. The chapter develops two
distinct and independently significant conceptual strands: the identification of metals and
non-metals by their observable characteristics, and the chemical changes that metals undergo
through reaction with oxygen, water, and acids.

The chapter begins by drawing students' attention to the diverse materials present in everyday
objects — copper wire, aluminium foil, iron tools, sulfur powder, and charcoal — and frames the
question of how these can be organised into meaningful categories. The answer lies in a coherent
set of physical and chemical criteria that the chapter proceeds to investigate.

Section 4.1 develops the physical properties of metals through hands-on activities. Malleability
is tested by striking samples of metal and non-metal (such as sulfur or charcoal) with a hammer:
metals spread out or flatten, while non-metals crumble or shatter. Sonority is explored by
dropping objects onto a hard surface: metals produce a characteristic ringing sound when struck,
while non-metals do not. Thermal conductivity is demonstrated by placing metal and non-metal
spoons in hot water and observing which transfers heat more quickly to the hand. Electrical
conductivity is tested by placing different materials into a circuit tester: metals complete the
circuit and light the lamp, while most non-metals do not. Students thereby identify the defining
physical properties of metals: lustre (shiny surface), hardness, malleability (can be beaten into
sheets), ductility (can be drawn into wires), sonority, and conductivity of heat and electricity.
Non-metals lack most of these properties and are brittle, dull, and poor conductors.

Section 4.2 introduces the chemical properties of metals. Metals react with oxygen in air to form
metal oxides, which are generally basic in nature. Because these reactions often require heating to
high temperatures or involve hazardous substances (burning magnesium ribbon produces very bright
light; burning sulfur in air produces toxic sulfur dioxide), they are demonstrated by the teacher
rather than conducted by students directly. Students record and discuss the properties of the
products. Metals also react with water: reactive metals such as sodium react explosively with cold
water, producing hydrogen gas and a metal hydroxide. Less reactive metals require steam or acids
to react. Metals react with dilute acids to produce hydrogen gas and a salt. The chapter
introduces the concept of chemical reactivity: different metals react with varying degrees of
vigour, and not all metals react with all substances.

Section 4.2.1 addresses corrosion — the gradual deterioration of metals through chemical reaction
with their environment. The rusting of iron is the chapter's primary example: students set up a
long-term experiment with three iron nails under different conditions (dry air, sealed water, open
water exposed to air) and observe over several days which nail rusts most. Rust is identified as
hydrated iron oxide, formed by the reaction of iron with oxygen and water, and is a new substance
with different properties from iron itself.

Section 4.3 examines the properties of non-metals. Non-metals are generally poor conductors of
heat and electricity, are brittle, and lack metallic lustre. They exist in all three physical
states at room temperature: sulfur and phosphorus are solids; bromine is a liquid; oxygen,
nitrogen, hydrogen, and chlorine are gases. Non-metals typically form acidic oxides when they
react with oxygen — the reverse of the basic oxides formed by metals.

Section 4.4 connects the properties of metals and non-metals to their everyday uses. Metals are
valued for strength, ductility, and conductivity: iron in construction, copper in electrical
wiring, aluminium in aircraft and cooking vessels, gold and silver in jewellery. Non-metals serve
equally important roles: oxygen sustains respiration; nitrogen is used in fertilisers; carbon as
diamond is used in cutting tools and as graphite in pencils and electrodes; silicon underpins
modern electronics.

By the end, students can classify materials as metals or non-metals using physical and chemical
evidence, explain the chemical reactions metals undergo, and connect material properties to
practical applications in everyday life.
