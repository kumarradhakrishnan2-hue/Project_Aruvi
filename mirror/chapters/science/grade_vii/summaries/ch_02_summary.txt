Chapter 2 — Exploring Substances: Acidic, Basic, and Neutral

Chapter 2 introduces students to one of the fundamental classification frameworks in chemistry:
the systematic identification of substances as acidic, basic, or neutral based on their chemical
characteristics. The chapter establishes this classification through direct testing using chemical
indicators and then develops a second major conceptual strand — the neutralisation reaction — as a
chemical change with significant real-world applications.

The chapter opens by drawing attention to the common experience of tasting sour foods such as
lemon juice, tamarind, and amla, establishing that a class of substances called acids exists in
the world around us, and that sourness is one recognisable characteristic of acidic substances.
Students then encounter the concept of bases — substances that feel soapy or slippery to touch
and are associated with cleansers and certain medicines. A third category, neutral substances,
exhibits neither the properties of acids nor of bases.

Section 2.1 introduces litmus as the most familiar chemical indicator, explaining that it is
extracted from lichen and available as red litmus and blue litmus paper or solution. Acids turn
blue litmus red, bases turn red litmus blue, and neutral substances produce no colour change.
Students learn that an indicator is any substance that changes colour in response to the acidic or
basic nature of a solution and is therefore used as a diagnostic tool.

Section 2.2 expands the toolkit by introducing natural indicators prepared from plant materials.
Red rose petals, when boiled in water, produce a coloured extract that turns pink or red in acidic
solutions and green or yellow in basic ones. Turmeric, used as turmeric paper or solution, turns
reddish-brown in the presence of a base but shows no change with acids. The chapter also
introduces olfactory indicators — substances whose smell changes distinctly in the presence of
acids or bases. Onion extract loses its characteristic smell in basic solutions; vanilla extract
behaves similarly. The inclusion of olfactory indicators broadens the range of testing methods
beyond colour change and reinforces that chemical indicators can operate through multiple sensory
modalities. A series of embedded activities asks students to systematically test a range of
household substances — lemon juice, baking soda solution, common salt solution, washing soda,
and tamarind water — using each type of indicator in turn, recording results in observation tables
and arriving at the classification of each substance.

Section 2.3 introduces the neutralisation reaction, which occurs when an acid and a base are
combined. The two react to form a salt and water, and the reaction releases heat. The word
equation is stated clearly: Acid + Base → Salt + Water + Heat. When the acid and base are
combined in exactly the right proportions, the product is a neutral salt solution that changes
neither red nor blue litmus. An embedded activity demonstrates this by gradually adding lemon
juice (an acid) to lime water (a base) and monitoring the colour change of the indicator at
regular intervals.

Section 2.3.1 situates neutralisation in everyday life. Ant bites and bee stings introduce formic
acid into the skin; applying a mild base such as baking soda or calamine lotion relieves the pain
by neutralising the acid. Acidic soil is corrected for farming by adding lime, a base, to bring
the pH closer to neutral. Industrial waste containing strong acids can be treated with bases
before being discharged into water bodies, reducing environmental harm. Antacid tablets used to
relieve excess stomach acidity operate through the same chemical principle.

By the end of the chapter, students can classify substances using multiple indicator methods,
explain the neutralisation reaction in terms of reactants, products, and observable evidence of
the reaction's occurrence, and connect the chemistry of acids and bases to contexts in food,
health, agriculture, and environmental management.
