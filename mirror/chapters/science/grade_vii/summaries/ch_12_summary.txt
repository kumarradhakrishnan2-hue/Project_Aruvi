Chapter 12 — Earth, Moon, and the Sun

Chapter 12 examines the celestial system consisting of the Earth, the Moon, and the Sun,
explaining the motions of these bodies and the phenomena those motions produce: the daily cycle
of day and night, the annual cycle of seasons, the phases of the Moon, and the dramatic events
of solar and lunar eclipses. The chapter connects the geometry of celestial positions and
movements to everyday and seasonal experience as well as to the cultural significance of
astronomical observation.

Section 12.1 begins with the Earth's rotation on its own axis — the motion that produces the
daily alternation of day and night. Students investigate apparent motion first: sitting on a
merry-go-round and watching the surroundings appear to move in the opposite direction illustrates
how an observer who is moving may perceive stationary objects as moving. Applied to the Earth,
this means that as the Earth rotates from west to east, the Sun, Moon, and stars appear to move
from east to west across the sky. Students use a globe and a sticker to model day and night:
rotating the globe toward a torch (representing the Sun), they observe when their location faces
the light (day) and when it is in shadow (night). The Earth completes one rotation in
approximately 24 hours. An embedded field activity asks students to observe the positions of the
Big Dipper and the Pole Star at different times during an evening, directly experiencing the
apparent westward drift of the star field due to the Earth's eastward rotation.

Section 12.2 addresses the Earth's annual revolution around the Sun, which takes approximately
365.25 days. The Earth's rotational axis is tilted at approximately 23.5° relative to the plane
of its orbit. This tilt, maintained in a fixed orientation as the Earth revolves, causes
different hemispheres to receive sunlight at different angles and for different durations at
different times of the year — the cause of the seasons. When the Northern Hemisphere is tilted
toward the Sun, it receives more direct sunlight for longer periods, producing summer; the
Southern Hemisphere experiences winter simultaneously. Six months later the situation is
reversed. The chapter introduces the summer solstice (the date when the Northern Hemisphere has
the longest day), the winter solstice (the shortest day), and the two equinoxes (when day and
night are of equal length everywhere on Earth). The chapter also explains the phases of the Moon:
as the Moon revolves around the Earth approximately once every 29.5 days, the fraction of its
sunlit face visible from the Earth changes progressively through new Moon, waxing crescent, first
quarter, waxing gibbous, full Moon, waning gibbous, last quarter, and waning crescent phases.
The Moon does not emit its own light but reflects sunlight. The lunar month formed the basis of
traditional calendar systems used across many cultures.

Section 12.3 addresses eclipses. A solar eclipse occurs when the Moon moves into a position
directly between the Earth and the Sun, casting a shadow on a part of the Earth's surface. An
embedded activity demonstrates apparent size: a thumb held at arm's length can block a friend's
distant head from view, just as the Moon — though much smaller than the Sun — can completely
block the Sun's disc when the geometry is right, because the Moon is much closer to the Earth.
In the region of the Moon's umbra (total shadow), a total solar eclipse occurs, with the Sun's
corona momentarily visible. In the wider penumbra, a partial eclipse is experienced. Because the
Moon's shadow is small, a total solar eclipse is visible only from a narrow track on the Earth's
surface at any one time. The chapter includes important safety guidance: the Sun must never be
viewed directly, even during a partial eclipse; safe methods include projecting the Sun's image
through a pinhole or reflecting it from a plane mirror onto a white screen.

A lunar eclipse occurs when the Earth comes between the Sun and the Moon so that the Earth's
shadow falls on the Moon. Because the Earth is significantly larger than the Moon, it is possible
for the Moon to be completely immersed in the Earth's umbra, producing a total lunar eclipse.
During totality the Moon may appear deep red, coloured by sunlight refracted through the Earth's
atmosphere and bent into the shadow. Lunar eclipses are visible from the entire night side of the
Earth, in contrast to the limited visibility strip of total solar eclipses.

The chapter concludes by noting that ancient Indian astronomers recorded and calculated eclipses
with remarkable accuracy, exemplifying the long history of systematic astronomical observation in
India and connecting scientific content to its cultural and historical context.
